Program Updates

    • Crystal Zang ’20 earned her degree from Pittsburgh and took a job with Johnson & Johnson

  • Speaking of the decennial review, can you believe it’s been ten years?! Here is how the report starts:

In short, the faculty of the Statistics and Data Science Program are passionate, dedicated individuals who are providing an impressive learning experience for the students of Smith. Inspecting the wider ecology of U.S. liberal arts colleges, Smith’s SDS Program has few peers, and is itself widely respected as an innovator and a trend setter. When one reviewer was designing a similar major at his liberal arts college, he kept returning to Smith’s program for inspiration in curriculum design and student experience. Similarly, via our discussions with students and faculty members outside of SDS at Smith, it became clear that the program has also garnered great respect within Smith.

Furthermore, the sabbatical allowed me to make progress on several other research projects:

    • Educational Opportunities of Participatory GIS for Accessibility on a College Campus with Heather Rosenfeld and Sarah Susnea ’25
    • Examining the Intersectional and Structural Issues of Routine Healthcare Utilization and Access Inequities for LGB People with Chronic Diseases with Mehreen Merza ’25, Sophia Silovsky ’24, Nicole Tresvalles ’24, Lucia Qin ’26, and Sarah Susnea ’25. This paper stems from a project paper Mehreen Merza ’25, Sophia Silovsky ’24, and Nicole Tresvalles ’24 wrote for my SDS 300: Disability Inclusion and Data Analytics in Fall 2023

This year I wrapped up my three-year term as chair of SDS. It was an eventful year with a search for a new professor joint with GOV (hiring Sara Kirshbaum) and our first ever decennial review, to name a few highlights. It was a pleasure to serve as chair of SDS, but I’m happy to pass the baton back to Ben’s capable hands!

I’m happy to now turn my attention primarily back to teaching and research. My collaborations with Sarah Witkowski and Stephanie Jones in the Exercise and Sport Studies department here at Smith have progressed. Prof Witkowski’s work investigates on perimenopause symptoms, activity, and cardiovascular health while Prof Jones studies multiple sclerosis, somatosensory impairments, and fatigue. Witkowski and I published a paper in Physiological Reports this year with Tint Tha Ra Wun ’25. Jones’s grant is close to funded! Cross your fingers for us.

Maya Rosen, Smith neuroscience professor, and I revised a paper for the journal Developmental Psychopathology with Lizeth Sepulveda ’25 investigating childhood executive functioning as a buffer against declines in mental health due to COVID-19 stressors.

I have also begun two research collaborations leveraging my expertise in dyadic data analysis. One is in collaboration with Amy Hale and Rachael Coakley at Boston Children’s Hospital and the Harvard Medical School assessing the effectiveness of parent-child interventions to reduced children’s chronic pain. The second is on a project working with Frederick Altice and David Oliveros at Yale School of Medicine investigating a dyadic behavioral design intervention to reduce stigma in populations affected by injection drug use.

I have also continued working on research in my lab, revising papers on the division of household labor, gatekeeping, and social dominance orientation with Meredith Zhang ’23 (submitted to the Journal of Family Psychology), as well as a paper on self-objectification in online learning contexts with Jessica Pardim Araujo ’23.

This year I had five Smith students in my research group, three are SDS students: Kayla Tillmon ’27, Nora Zhou ’26J, and Itzel Aleman Flores ’27. We are now investigating political polarization and approach and avoidance of political conversatoins depending on relationship closeness. I’m looking forward to continuing my research in the fall!

In psychology I taught two sections of PSY 202 Research Methods this past fall. Then, this past semester I taught SDS 210 Introduction to Statistics for the first time in 8 years, coordinating with Ben Baumer. A lot has changed in the world! I learning A LOT — thank you, Ben! I am excited to teach SDS 100 for the first time in Fall 2026.

This past year has been incredibly eventful and rewarding! I am absolutely delighted to have been reappointed at Smith during my third-year review. On the teaching front, I had the pleasure of teaching Time Series Analysis for the first time—a dream course with a fantastic group of students.

My research side was equally busy. My R package, fixedCV, was officially published on CRAN, a corresponding paper was accepted with revisions, and two more are on track to wrap up by the end of summer. My work this year focused on robust variance estimation in time series—essentially finding ways to make sure our statistical conclusions remain valid even when we know very little about the data. I also co-authored two book chapters: one for the second edition of the Handbook of Markov Chain Monte Carlo, and another on online learning tools for introductory statistics.

Off-campus, my partner and I are eagerly preparing to welcome our first child at the end of the summer! In a flurry of nesting and tackling my to-do list, I somehow managed to complete five quilts. While I’d normally enter my work into the Big E or the Three-County Fair, this year’s batch is happily destined straight for nurseries—both my own and those of loved ones!

Between crafting a new course, writing research papers, stitching together quilts, and preparing to welcome a brand-new human, it’s been a massive year of growing and creating!

Lindsay Poirier

This past year has been one of many gratifying transitions for me. Closing out a long-standing ethnography of the semantic web, I published a paper in a Social Studies of Science, examining social action involved in formalizing owl:sameas - a single property in an ontology language. The paper explores how computational practitioners negotiate and redefine the meaning of sameness and difference as they attempt to stabilize standards for modeling data.

Exploring some new directions for my research, in Fall 2025, I was a fellow in the Kahn Institute’s long-term project on Hauntings. Unearthing stories that haunt our statistics, I opted to focus on a project exploring the social history of the maternal mortality rate in the U.S. - examining how its reporting has shaped social hierarchies in medicine, been operationalized to justify stances on family planning, and been leveraged to make sense of the state of the country’s healthcare system. I look forward to continuing work on this project in the coming years.

I also published a paper in the Journal of Statistics and Data Science Education motivating and outlining how I teach Data Ethnography at Smith. In addition to discussing how students learn to study the data science community anthropologically, the article also highlights the exemplary work of several Smith students.

While the news of my promotion to associate professor was certainly a highlight of the past year, nothing brought me quite as much joy as welcoming a little one into my family. My son Rafi was born in December, and I’ve spent the past seven months stunned by how much fuller life can feel with him.
